## 2. What "epic, better than lederne and aka" means in 2026

The legacy orgs are excellent but built for native Danes who already understand the system. Six things make a 2026 offering categorically better. These are the design principles for InDe:

1. **Built for the international, not retrofitted.** Every legacy service silently assumes you read Danish, have a CPR, understand a-kasse, and have a network. InDe's version assumes none of that. The contract review explains Danish employment law to someone who has never seen it. The salary tool says "here is what your foreign degree is worth here." This is the entire moat.
2. **Digital-first and self-serve, human when it counts.** Legacy orgs make you phone during office hours. InDe should be answerable 24/7 by an honestly-labeled AI assistant for the 80% of questions that are factual ("how do I renew my permit"), and route the 20% that need judgment (a contract dispute, a termination) to a human. The Danish sector itself is now asking "which tasks must never be automated because they are the core democratic task." InDe answers that line clearly.
3. **Proactive, not reactive.** Legacy orgs wait for you to call. InDe should reach out: "your permit expires in 90 days, here is the checklist," "salary statistics for your role just updated." Timeline-and-trigger based, because internationals miss deadlines that locals never see.
4. **Personalized to your journey stage.** A newcomer in month 1, a professional in year 3 deciding whether to stay, a spouse who cannot find work, a founder. The same org, different doors.
5. **Community-powered, peer-to-peer.** Legacy networks are professional. InDe's superpower is internationals helping internationals plus Danes welcoming them. Mentorship, peer circles, "someone who has done this before." Cheap to run, high in trust, and it is what InDe already does (pro-bono coaching, Meetups).
6. **Radically transparent and honest.** Label what is live now vs aspirational. No fake numbers. State who delivers each service (volunteer, partner, pro-bono lawyer). This is the trust signal that converts a grassroots org into an institution-grade platform, which is InDe's explicit strategic need.

# InDe Needs Analysis: Ranked Pain Points of Internationals in Denmark and the Service White Space

Prepared for the InDe member-services design round. All figures are sourced and dated. Where a number is uncertain or aspirational it is labeled as such.

## The headline problem InDe exists to solve

Nearly half of internationals in the Copenhagen Capital Region leave within five years, and the departure rate has risen roughly 60% over the last three years. In 2024, 52,580 internationals left Denmark, the second-highest number on record, and retention kept falling into 2025. The single most cited reason is not money or weather. It is **not feeling included at work** and **not building social connection**. Denmark is good at attracting talent and bad at keeping it. That retention gap is InDe's reason to exist, and it is the thread that should run through every service.

Crucially, the existing system already does the **transactional** onboarding well. International House Copenhagen handles CPR, MitID, tax card, residence documents, and even a-kasse (U2) registration, free, in English, under one roof, for Copenhagen plus 37 partner municipalities. So InDe should **not** rebuild bureaucracy help as its core. The unmet need is everything that happens **after** the paperwork: the job, the belonging, the protection when things go wrong, and a collective voice. That is the white space.

---

## Ranked needs (highest leverage first)

### 1. Job-market access and the hidden job market
**Why it ranks first.** Breaking into the workforce is the number one challenge. The market runs on informal channels: only about 16% of surveyed internationals got their role from a job advert, while ~40% got it through LinkedIn and networking. An international without a Danish network is locked out of most of the actual market, which is never posted. This compounds every other problem, because in Denmark the job is also the visa, the income security, and the main route to belonging.

**The service that moves the needle: a member-to-member job network and warm-referral engine, not a job board.** Job boards are everywhere and they are the part that does not work here. InDe should run structured introductions: members who are hiring or who work at hiring companies, matched to job-seeking members; "bring one international into your network" drives; a referrals channel; recurring CV and application clinics; mock interviews; and LinkedIn-optimisation sessions delivered by volunteer professionals and partners (Copenhagen Capacity, Dansk Industri). The deliverable is access to the hidden market, which is exactly what no public service provides.

### 2. The job-loss-equals-visa-risk trap (and a-kasse that does not actually protect non-EU members)
**Why it ranks this high.** For non-EU/non-EEA members this is existential, and it is the cleanest white space of all. Two facts collide:
- A work-permit holder who loses their job typically gets a **6-month job-seeking window** to find a new qualifying role or lose the right to stay. Losing a job is losing your residence clock.
- A-kasse looks like a safety net but often is not one for them. To draw unemployment benefit (dagpenge) you must be able to **take any job on one day's notice**, which requires a **general work permit not tied to one employer**. Many non-EU permits are employer- or field-specific, so the member pays a-kasse dues for years and is still ineligible to claim. Plus the standard bars: 1 year of membership and roughly DKK 286,632 of income across 3 years.

No Danish union or a-kasse is built to explain this intersection, because their members are mostly Danes for whom the permit issue does not exist. This is InDe's to own.

**The service that moves the needle: a plain-language "Permit and Safety Net" clinic and a rapid-response path for members who lose a job.** Free guidance on whether your specific permit makes a-kasse worth paying for, what the 6-month clock means, how to read your residence card, and a checklist for the first 14 days after a layoff (renewal prep, job-seeking permit, who to call). Delivered by trained volunteers and pro-bono legal partners, with clear "we guide, we do not represent you legally" labelling. This single service could be the most loved thing InDe does.

### 3. Spousal and trailing-partner employment
**Why it ranks here.** This is a top-three reason families leave, and it is under-served by everyone. Only about half of partners who relocated have found work, versus ~85% of the primary movers. 27% of internationals who leave early do so because their partner is unhappy, and 33% said better career options for the partner would have made them stay. In one IT study, two in five professionals left within a year because the partner could not find work. The partner is highly educated, often on a dependent permit, and completely outside any career system.

**The service that moves the needle: a dedicated partner track inside the job network.** Treat the trailing partner as a first-class member, not an add-on. Partner-specific networking, peer cohorts, sector reintroduction sessions, and confidence-and-restart coaching, because many partners have a career gap and a self-worth hit, not just a job gap. Pair this with the pro-bono coaching InDe already runs.

### 4. Belonging, loneliness, and making Danish connections
**Why it ranks here.** This is the emotional core of the retention problem and the most cited "soft" driver of leaving. Internationals describe watching Danish social life "through a window." Danish friendships form early through school and clubs and are hard to enter as an adult. An expat survey once ranked Danes the hardest nationality in the world to befriend. The Copenhagen Post now runs whole series on international loneliness and the rise in internationals seeking therapy. More than half of internationals say meeting Danes is difficult and that more structured social opportunities would have improved their stay.

**The service that moves the needle: structured belonging, not more open mixers.** InDe already runs InDe Meetup and InDe Connect. Elevate them into something with retention power: a buddy or mentor pairing that intentionally connects internationals with Danes and with settled internationals; small recurring interest-based circles (the thing that actually builds friendship); and a peer-support layer with clear signposting to professional mental-health help. The word internationals use is "structured." Give them structure.

### 5. Workplace culture and the unwritten rules
**Why it ranks here.** "Did not feel included at work" is the top stated reason for leaving, and it is mostly about culture, not contracts: flat hierarchy, consensus, indirectness, the social world of the team defaulting to Danish, after-work norms. Employers who actively support inclusion retain people; most do not know how.

**The service that moves the needle: a two-sided culture programme.** For members, short practical workshops on navigating the Danish workplace, feedback culture, and reading the unwritten rules. For employers (a partnership and future-revenue angle), an "international-ready workplace" briefing. This turns a member need into an institutional relationship, which directly serves InDe's "institution-grade platform" goal.

### 6. Foreign-degree and professional-credential recognition
**Why it ranks here.** A real barrier, but more navigable than the top five because a free public path exists. The Danish Agency for Higher Education and Science assesses foreign qualifications free in 1 to 2 months, and public employers must accept the stated level. The friction is in the ~120 regulated/authorised professions (health, law, teaching, some engineering) where access can be very limited and a few roles even require Danish citizenship. Members often do not know the assessment exists, or do not know if their profession is regulated.

**The service that moves the needle: a credential-recognition wayfinder.** A simple guided tool plus clinic: is your profession regulated, which authority to contact, how to request the free assessment, and how to position a foreign degree to a Danish employer. Low cost, high relief, and it routes members into the existing free system rather than duplicating it.

### 7. Danish language access
**Why it ranks lower than its reputation.** It is a genuine long-run barrier to deep belonging and to the regulated professions, but it is the most served need: municipal Danish courses exist and most workplaces run in English. For InDe, language is best treated as **support and motivation around** the public courses, not as a course provider.

**The service that moves the needle:** conversation circles and study buddies that lower the social cost of practising, plus signposting and accountability for the official courses. Cheap, social, and it doubles as a belonging activity.

### 8. CPR, MitID, Digital Post, SKAT and core bureaucracy
**Why it ranks last for InDe specifically.** Not because it is easy (it is genuinely hard and frightening for newcomers) but because International House and ICS already deliver it well and free. InDe duplicating it would burn scarce volunteer capacity on a solved problem.

**The service that moves the needle:** do not rebuild it. Offer a **curated, trustworthy signpost** (a simple "first 30 days" wayfinder pointing to the real official channels, plus peer Q&A) and warm hand-offs to International House. This builds trust cheaply and keeps InDe out of a lane it cannot win.

### Housing and deposits
A real and painful problem (scarce rentals, large deposits, scam exposure), but largely structural and market-wide, hard for a small volunteer org to move directly, and tied to InDe's housing-strategy advocacy point rather than a member service. **Best handled as advocacy plus a scam-and-deposit safety briefing and peer warnings, not as a brokering service.**

## The white space, stated plainly: what no Danish union or a-kasse does for internationals

The reference orgs (lederne.dk, aka.dk, djoef.dk, ida.dk) are excellent at career advice, salary tools, legal/contract review, and a-kasse income security, **for people who already belong**. Their entire model assumes you have the right to live and work indefinitely, you have a network, you read Danish, and your degree is recognised. Every one of those assumptions is false for an international. So the gaps InDe can own:

1. **Nobody connects the permit, the job, and the safety net into one picture.** A Dane's a-kasse is simple. An international's a-kasse is a trap unless someone explains the permit-type and one-day-notice rule first. This integrated "career plus immigration plus income security" guidance is the flagship white space.
2. **Nobody gives internationals access to the hidden job market.** Unions help you negotiate the job you already found in a market you already understand. InDe's network can help you find a job in a market that hides 80% of itself.
3. **Nobody treats the trailing partner as a member.** The partner is invisible to every existing system. InDe can make them visible.
4. **Nobody builds structured belonging.** A-kasses do not do friendship. The retention data says friendship and inclusion are exactly what decide whether people stay.
5. **Nobody combines services with collective voice.** Because InDe also has an 8-point policy agenda, it can do what no a-kasse does: fix the individual problem this month and push to remove the systemic barrier this year. "We help you through it, and we work to make sure the next person does not have to face it." That dual promise is InDe's distinctive identity.

A clean way to package this for the showcase page is the through-line **"From your first day to your whole working life, and a voice that outlasts any one of us."** Three honest layers: **Settle** (wayfinding and trust, lightweight, signpost the free public services), **Thrive** (the real value: jobs, partner careers, belonging, culture, credentials, language, coaching), and **Protect** (the permit-and-safety-net clinic and rapid response when a job is lost). All free or pro-bono delivered, with anything aspirational clearly labeled "coming soon."

## 5. MOTION LANGUAGE

Motion is calm and confident, never bouncy. Easing is mostly a gentle ease-out so things "arrive and settle" like a drawer closing softly. Default `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)` (a soft, decisive ease-out) as `--ease-out`; `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)` as `--ease` for hovers.

- **Page intro (on load):** a 1-step staggered reveal of the hero. Eyebrow, then headline, then subhead, then CTA, each rising `16px` and fading in, `420ms` per element, `90ms` stagger, `--ease-out`. The hero illustration fades and scales from `0.98` to `1` over `700ms`. Total intro under `1.1s`. No spinner, no splash.
- **Scroll reveal:** sections and cards fade up `24px` as they enter, via IntersectionObserver adding an `.in` class. `560ms`, `--ease-out`, with a `60ms` stagger across a card row (CSS `transition-delay` keyed by `--i` index). Reveal triggers at `rootMargin: "0px 0px -12% 0px"` so it fires slightly before fully in view.
- **Hover:** cards lift `-4px` and raise elevation over `220ms` `--ease`. Buttons darken fill and nudge an inline arrow `4px` over `180ms`. Links draw an underline from start to end (`background-size` trick) over `260ms`. All subtle.
- **Signature moment (see section 7):** the "Welcome Desk" lamp glow and the door-open reveal, detailed below. ~`900ms`, `--ease-out`.
- **Focus:** focus ring appears instantly (no transition on focus, accessibility), a `3px` `--accent` outline with `2px` offset.

**prefers-reduced-motion plan:** wrap ALL of the above in `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: no-preference)`. Under reduced-motion: no transforms, no fades, no stagger; elements are simply present at full opacity. Crucially also zero out `animation-delay` and `transition-delay` (the lesson from the ankommer/sector-debrief work: staggered elements otherwise sit invisible waiting on their delay). The IntersectionObserver still runs but only adds the class with no visual transition. The signature lamp shows a static lit state, no flicker.

## 7. THE SIGNATURE COMPONENT / MOMENT

**"The Welcome Desk" services counter, with the lamp that lights as you arrive.**

The whole services section is staged as a single long counter (the institutional metaphor made literal). On top sits the one memorable interaction:

Built cheaply: one absolutely-positioned `<div class="lamp-glow">` with layered radial-gradients, animated with `@keyframes lampOn` (transform scale + opacity), and a CSS custom property `--glow` toggled per active card. ## 8. ACCESSIBILITY, RTL, AND VANILLA BUILDABILITY

**Accessibility (target WCAG 2.1 AA):**
- Every text/background pair in section 2 was chosen to clear AA (4.5:1 body, 3:1 large/UI); the few accent-on-paper pairs are confined to large text and UI plus always carry a non-color cue (underline on links, icon on status). Body links never rely on color alone.
- Semantic landmarks: one `<header>`, `<main>`, `<nav>`, `<footer>`; a visible skip link; logical heading order (single h1, then h2 per section, h3 per card).
- Focus visible everywhere (the `3px --accent` ring), keyboard operable for any interactive element, `:focus-visible` so mouse users do not see rings but keyboard users do.
- Motion fully gated behind `prefers-reduced-motion` per section 5, including delays.
- Images have real `alt`; decorative grain and the lamp glow are `aria-hidden="true"`.
- Plain, short English in all copy. Eyebrow mono labels also exposed as normal text (not letter-spaced into unreadability for screen readers, which read the source text fine).
- Color is theme-toggleable; the toggle persists in `localStorage` and respects `prefers-color-scheme` on first visit, set before first paint via a tiny inline script to avoid a flash (the proven ankommer pattern).

**RTL-friendly:**
- Layout uses CSS logical properties throughout, so `dir="rtl"` on `<html>` mirrors the entire page (counter, cards, lamp position, arrows) with no rewrites. Lucide arrows flip via a `[dir="rtl"] .icon-arrow { transform: scaleX(-1); }` rule. Font swaps via `:lang()` as noted. The hero negative space is defined with `padding-inline` so the headline space moves to the correct side automatically.

**Vanilla static buildability:**
- Single `index.html` plus `css/style.css` plus `js/main.js`. No framework, no build step, no bundler. Runs on `python -m http.server` or any static host.
- All design tokens live as CSS custom properties on `:root` and `[data-theme="dark"]`, so the entire identity is themeable from one place (and a single `--accent` change re-vibes the page, matching Ali's supabase-starter habit).
- JS is ~3 small responsibilities only: theme toggle, IntersectionObserver scroll-reveal + lamp tracking, and the mobile nav. Everything else is CSS. Total JS well under 6KB.
- Fonts via Google Fonts `<link>` with `display=swap`; preconnect to fonts.gstatic.com; the FLUX illustrations are local JPEGs; icons are inline Lucide SVG (no icon-font request). Fully offline-capable and fast.
